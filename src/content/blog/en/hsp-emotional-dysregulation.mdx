Many people assume emotional volatility is just a lack of "willpower" or being "too dramatic."

In neurobiology, emotional dysregulation is a common physiological trait of ADHD & HSP brains.

Our prefrontal cortex has a weaker inhibitory filter over the amygdala (the emotional center).

This means minor annoyances that others filter out pass through our brains unhindered, blowing up into existential threats. Your body goes into a high-pressure "fight or flight" mode, freezing your executive function.

Stop blaming yourself for being "emotional." You need somatic **rhythm tracking** coupled with **cognitive unloading**.

---

## The Somatic-Cognitive Landing Routine

When an emotional wave hits, your rational brain is offline. Intervene from physical and cognitive angles:

### 1. Somatic Port: Cool Down with the Breath Orb
During a storm, your body experiences real physical changes: racing heart, shallow breathing, chest tightness. Thinking at this stage is adding fuel to the fire.
Disconnect from inputs and open the [Breath Orb](https://adhdos.app/breath-orb) in ADHDOS.
Close your eyes and breathe in sync with the visual, rhythmic expansion of the orb for 1 to 2 minutes.
This somatic breathing sends safety signals to your amygdala, lowering your body's alarm state so reason can return.

### 2. Cognitive Port: Dismantle Doom-Thinking with the Reframer
Once your body cools down, your brain will still register negative predictions.
Open the [Cognitive Reframer](https://adhdos.app/reframer) in ADHDOS to separate emotional fear from objective reality:
*   **Panic Brain**: "I forgot my umbrella. Today is ruined. I mess everything up."
*   **Reframed Reality**: "Forgetting an umbrella is just a probability. Buying one for $10 or waiting 10 minutes solves it. My life is not ruined."
*   **Panic Brain**: "Their tone was cold. I must have offended them. I am terrible at this."
*   **Reframed Reality**: "Other people's moods are their own business. Their coldness could be fatigue or work stress. It has no bearing on my worth."

Use cognitive reframing as a shield to stop your brain from confusing feelings with reality.
