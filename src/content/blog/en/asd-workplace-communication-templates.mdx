Your manager sends a message in the team chat: *"Please sync up on your active project status before logging off."*

Looking at the message, your brain instantly triggers a high-energy alarm. You stare at the screen, caught in a loop of indecision: *Does 'status' mean exact code changes, or a general percentage? Should I mention my current blocks? What tone sounds proactive without sounding boastful?* You type, delete, and rewrite for 30 minutes. Meanwhile, your coworker spends two minutes sending three lines of text. By the time you send your message, you feel exhausted.

This is not a sign of bad communication skills. For autistic (ASD) adults, office interactions demand running a high-cost social simulation algorithm.

## The Neurobiology of Workplace Socializing: The Double Empathy Problem

In psychology and developmental sciences, the **Double Empathy Problem** explains that autistic individuals and neurotypicals (NT) do not have communication "deficits"—they run entirely different information encoding systems:

1.  **The NT Implicit Network**: Neurotypicals possess intuitive pathways for social cues. They default to implicit hints, shifts in tone, and non-verbal subtext. Processing these messages demands zero conscious energy.
2.  **The Autistic Factual Network**: The autistic brain defaults to precise, fact-based communication. Vague directions like *"sync up"* or *"use your judgment"* force the prefrontal cortex to run heavy **Social Cognition algorithms** to guess the implied meaning.

Simulating NT communication on an autistic neural architecture burns your active RAM, leading to social burnout.

## Structured Communication: Visual Filtering and Card Templates

To bypass this friction, stop trying to perform as a neurotypical social expert. Treat your daily communication as a physical process by using templates to enable **cognitive unloading**.

### 1. The 3-Step Status Update Template
When asked for a status update, do not write paragraphs. Use this fact-based format:
*   **[Completed]**: List 1-2 objective deliverables (e.g., Finished draft of design file A).
*   **[In Progress]**: List current tasks and target delivery dates (e.g., Auditing metrics sheet, expecting delivery Thursday afternoon).
*   **[Blocked/Need Help]**: List blockers, or write None (e.g., Awaiting user metrics from team B).
This clean, factual structure reads as professional and organized to NT managers, saving your energy.

### 2. Pinned Board Shields: Copy, Paste, and Move On
Requesting time off, following up on approvals, or declining scope creep are friction-heavy tasks.
Keep standard card templates pinned to your ADHDOS [Board](https://adhdos.app/board). **Forbid drafting responses under pressure; copy-paste only**:
*   **Follow-Up**: *"Hi [Name], I am working on project A. Could you let me know when approval on project B is expected? This timeline dictates when we launch phase two. Thanks!"*
*   **Time Off**: *"Hi [Manager], I plan to take Friday off. I will review urgent messages on Slack once at 5 PM that day. Thanks for approving!"*

Using these pinned card templates unloads the cognitive cost of phrasing, preserving your battery.
