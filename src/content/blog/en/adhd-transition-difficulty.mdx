Many people assume ADHD is just an "inability to focus."

In truth, we can focus intensely—sometimes too much. Our real struggle is **moving our focus**.

The ADHD brain is like a heavy truck without a clutch. Forcing it to slam its brakes on a high-dopamine activity (like gaming) and shift instantly to a low-dopamine one (like chores) causes physical distress.

To avoid this, our prefrontal cortex drags out the previous activity, leading to painful procrastination.

We must use **rhythm tracking** and **elastic focus** to create transition ramps.

---

## 3 Steps to Transition Task Friction-Free

Don't expect your brain to shift gears instantly. Build a psychological buffer:

### 1. Set a Visual-Physical Buffer: The Focus Clock
Don't use willpower or loud alarms.
Use the [Focus Clock](https://adhdos.app/focus-clock) in ADHDOS. It visualizes the passage of time on a clean progress bar.
As the bar runs down, your brain visually prepares to disengage. This is much gentler than a sudden alarm.

### 2. Shift to Neutral with the Breath Orb
When you close your laptop or turn off the TV, don't force yourself to start work immediately.
Open the [Breath Orb](https://adhdos.app/breath-orb) in ADHDOS. Take 3 to 5 deep breaths matching its contraction.
This physically shifts your autonomic nervous system, pulling your brain out of the old task's neural pathway into "neutral."

### 3. Warm Up with a Micro-Action
Once in neutral, perform a low-resistance start action.
Instead of "drafting a report," tell yourself to "type 5 random words."
Use warm-up tasks to build momentum. Once the wheels spin, momentum will shift you into the new task.
