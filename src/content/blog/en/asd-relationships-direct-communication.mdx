"I'm fine."
When a neurotypical partner says this, it might mean: "I am actually not fine. I want you to stop working, come over, hug me, and ask me what's wrong."

But when an **autistic** person hears it, their brain interprets it literally: "Oh, she said she's fine. I can go back to what I was doing."

What follows is deeper frustration from one partner, and confusion and guilt from the other.

This misalignment is a primary source of conflict in neurodiverse relationships.

The non-autistic partner feels the other is cold and robotic, while the autistic partner feels they are constantly taking an exam without being given the questions.

To bridge this gap, we don't need to force ourselves to learn complex mind-reading tricks. Instead, we must establish a mutual **double translation protocol**.

---

## 1. Why We Miss Your Subtext

Autistic brains process language with a strong **literal thinking bias**.

Over millennia, human societies have developed complex layers of communication—conveying intent through tone, facial cues, gestures, and subtext.

However, autistic individuals lack the neurological wiring to automatically decode these multi-channel signals.

If communication is a network, neurotypicals transmit encrypted data that is decrypted subconsciously, whereas autistic people require plaintext.

When you drop hints, the information is simply lost in transit.

This is not a lack of caring; it is a difference in our receivers.

---

## 2. Setting Up a Relationship "API Protocol"

Since hinting fails, we must define explicit communication interfaces (APIs) just like software:

### 💬 Protocol 1: Explicit Demand Declaration
Both partners agree to abandon hints and use **direct declarative communication**:
*   ~~"The floor is so dirty."~~ (Hint: Go sweep it) → **"I am exhausted today. Could you please sweep the living room floor right now?"** (Clear request & action)
*   ~~"The weather looks nice this weekend."~~ (Hint: Take me out) → **"I want to walk by the beach this Saturday. Can you book the tickets by Thursday?"**

### 💬 Protocol 2: Defining Red, Yellow, and Green States
When energy levels drop, use simple color codes to communicate cognitive bandwidth instantly, avoiding misunderstandings:
*   🔴 **Red State**: "I am in sensory overload/shutdown. Let me be. Do not ask questions."
*   🟡 **Yellow State**: "I am tired and low on social energy. I can talk, but please keep sentences short."
*   🟢 **Green State**: "I am regulated. Ready for deep conversations."

---

## 3. The Empathy Mode Dial

When a partner complains about work stress, an autistic person's natural response is to analyze the logic and offer quick fixes. Often, however, the partner just wants to be heard and comforted.

To prevent this mismatch, dial in your needs before starting the conversation:

> "I am about to vent. Can you set your setting to one of these three modes?"
> 1.  **Lurker Mode**: Just listen and agree. No advice needed.
> 2.  **Hug Mode**: I am sad. Just hold me physically. No words.
> 3.  **Advisor Mode**: I am in trouble. Help me analyze and find a solution.

By setting the mode, the autistic partner can play their role perfectly, eliminating conflicts caused by guessing.

Directness is not the enemy of romance. Stating your love and needs clearly is the most authentic, stable romance in the neurodivergent world.
